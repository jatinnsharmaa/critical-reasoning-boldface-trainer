Premise vs Conclusion sorter

Read the term, pick its bucket. Missed ones come back around.

Why these three buckets?

Picture a courtroom. The detective brings the exhibit: fact, data, evidence, research, proof, observation, a pattern already seen. Nobody argues with the exhibit — that's the non-challengeable premise.

The lawyer stands up with the argument: reason, justification, support, supposition, policy, a generalization about what's already true. You can push back on this one — that's the challengeable premise.

The judge hands down the verdict: opinion, claim, prediction, position, a generalization about what's coming. It's the forward-looking endpoint — that's the conclusion.

One trap: "pattern" and "generalization" each show up in two buckets depending on tense. Past or present observed → premise. Future or predicted → conclusion. That's a judgment call about context, not something drilling the word alone fixes.
